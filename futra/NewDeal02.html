An Image of the Great Depression
Photo by Dorothea Lange
Class Discussion Activity

Directions: Encourage everyone to offer answers to the questions. The more answers, the better the discussion, the better the learning!

Questions for pre-story discussion:
ÔWhat are you impressions of the photo knowing that this photo was taken during the Great Depression?
ÔWhat is this a picture of?
ÔWhat is the dominate emotion in this picture?
ÔDoes she look happy or sad?
ÔDoes she look tired too?
ÔWhat does her expression reveal?
ÔWhat do you think she is thinking about?
ÔWhat do her clothes look like?
ÔWhat are her two kids doing?
ÔDo they look dirty?
ÔWhy do you think she looks sad?

Photo by Dorothea Lange

The story behind the image:
Dorothea Lange was working for the Farm Security Administration in the mid-1930s.  Along with her colleagues Russell Lee and Roy Stryker, she documented the problems of farm tenancy (or occupancy) in images that would become classic.  This photo was taken at a pea-pickerÌs camp in Nipomo, California. She couldn't find any permanent work and that she could find was for very little money.  Later Lange recalled, ÏI saw and approached the hungry and desperate mother, as if drawn by a magnet.  I do not remember who I explained by presence or my camera to her, but I do remember she asked me no questions.  I made five exposures, working closer and closer . . . I did not ask her name or her history.  She told me her age, that she was thirty-two.  She said that they had been living on frozen vegetables from the surrounding fields, and birds that the children killed.  She had just sold the tires from her car to buy food.  There she sat in that lean-to tent with her children huddled around her, and seemed to know that my picture might help her, and so she helped me.  There was a sort of equality about it.Ó Quoted in Milton MeltzerÌs Dorothea Lange: A PhotographerÌs Life, NY:Farrar Straus Giroux, 1978.  Photograph from the Library of Congress. (Also see WomenÌs America, Kerber and De Hart)

Questions for post-story discussion:
ÔHow do you now see the woman?
ÔHow do you feel towards her?
ÔWould you want to help her if you could?
ÔWhat would you do to help her?
ÔWhat do you think Lange meant when she said: ÏThere she sat in that lean-to tent with her children huddled around her, and seemed to know that my picture might help her, and so she helped me.  There was a sort of equality about it.Ó
ÔIf you were running for President of the United States and you wanted her to vote for you, what would you promise her, what would you do? (Create jobs, help farmers...)
