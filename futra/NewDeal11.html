The REA
Sponge/Journal Activity or Book Report Questions

Directions: Have the excerpt either on the board, on the overhead, or as a handout for the students to do at the end of the lesson or the following day when the students first come to class.  In their journals, have them explore the excerpt below and the posed questions about the REA, The Year the Lights Came On, and the effect of electricity on the American people.  If you want, you can have your students read the entire book.  It is an enjoyable read for all high school age students.

Journal Writing Assignment: Pretend that you were one of the millions of Americans who got electricity as a result of the efforts of the REA.  How do you feel about this new ìluxuryî?  How will it change your life for the better and for the worse.

Questions to consider:  What is the author saying about the affect electricity had on people?  Was it all good?  Why does he use the word ìisolationî to describe the way people were living before they got electricity?

Foundation Information: The REA (Rural Electrification Administration) does not make or transmit electricity.  It is a government agency - one of FDRís New Deal programs - that provides financing programs and develops standards of cooperatives providing electricity to millions of Americans.
 
ìThe announcement was printed, front page, in The Eden County Garden.  The headline was: NEW POWER LINE TO BE TURNED ON.  The REA had completed its spider webbing and its hookups.  On a given date, at a given hour, the main switch, off somewhere beyond our imaginations, would be pushed, or pulled, in a ceremony featuring speakers and officials and a ribbon cutting (there was always a ribbon-cutting), and electricity would leap along the messenger lines to flood our home... 

We are easily deceived.  The REA changed our lives.  The REA made us more comfortable.  The REA also destroyed us.  Destroyed something - some intangible security people have always enjoyed in isolation.  The world came to our house on those shining, singing wires.  The world came in, intruding and changing, commanding us to obey its hypnotic lure.  The world came in like a torrent, pouring out of every motor and gadget and instant, fumeless light, and it changed us.î
Source: The Year the Lights Came On by Terry Kay, Athens, Georgia: University of Georgia Press, 1976. Pages: Authorís note, 283 Epilogue.
